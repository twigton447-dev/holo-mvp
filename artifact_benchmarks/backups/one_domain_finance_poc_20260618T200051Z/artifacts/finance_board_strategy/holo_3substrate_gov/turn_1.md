# Board Strategy Memo: AI Infrastructure Capacity Commitment

## Recommendation

**Recommend preserving optionality for the next six months rather than committing immediately to the full two-year reserved AI infrastructure contract.**

This is the better board-level decision based on the current fact pattern because:

1. **The two-year commitment is large relative to liquidity and runway risk.** Reserved capacity would cost **$9.6M per year for two years**, while the company has **$31M cash and no debt**. Finance warns that a full commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.
2. **The company does have a real reliability problem tied to revenue protection and expansion.** Gross margin has already fallen from **73% to 66%** over three quarters because inference usage increased faster than pricing changes, and Sales attributes **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** to AI feature reliability.
3. **The decision is not simply “buy or wait.”** The board should preserve flexibility now, while using the next six months to gather evidence against explicit trigger thresholds that would justify a later commitment.

In short: **do not sign the full two-year contract now.** Instead, run a six-month decision window with defined commercial, operational, and financial triggers. If those triggers are met, return to the board/CFO with a commitment recommendation.

---

## Decision Framing

The company is balancing two competing truths:

- **Reliability may defend revenue and expansion**, especially as enterprise customers ask for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.
- **A fixed two-year infrastructure obligation increases financial risk**, especially given the runway impact if growth slows.

The key board question is therefore not whether AI infrastructure matters. It clearly does. The question is whether the company has enough evidence **today** to justify locking in a two-year fixed cost structure.

Based on the provided context, the answer is **no, not yet**.

---

## Explicit Assumptions

Because the context is incomplete, the following assumptions are stated explicitly and should be validated during the six-month window:

1. **Reserved capacity primarily improves cost predictability and likely supports reliability**, but the context does not quantify the exact reliability improvement versus on-demand capacity.
2. **On-demand capacity remains available during the next six months**, albeit at roughly **1.35x reserved price at expected utilization** and with volatility risk.
3. **The $6M at-risk expansion pipeline is not yet lost revenue**; it is pipeline risk attributed by Sales to AI feature reliability.
4. **Enterprise requirements for data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments may require infrastructure changes**, but the context does not confirm that a full two-year reserved contract is the only way to meet them.
5. **Growth remains uncertain.** Finance explicitly warns that if growth slows, the full commitment materially compresses runway.
6. **No market forecast, customer-level commitments, or utilization forecast is available**, so the recommendation must emphasize staged decision-making rather than a deterministic ROI case.

## Decision Criteria

The board and CFO should evaluate the decision against five criteria:

### 1. Liquidity and runway protection
- Current cash is **$31M** with **no debt**.
- A full reserved commitment is **$19.2M total over two years**.
- Finance warns runway could fall from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.

**Implication:** Any decision that materially reduces strategic flexibility must clear a high bar.

### 2. Revenue defense and expansion support
- Sales attributes **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** to AI feature reliability.
- Enterprise customers are asking for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.

**Implication:** Reliability and enterprise-readiness are commercially material, not just technical nice-to-haves.

### 3. Gross margin trajectory
- Gross margin declined from **73% to 66%** over three quarters because inference usage increased faster than pricing changes.
- On-demand capacity costs roughly **1.35x reserved price at expected utilization**.

**Implication:** The current AI cost structure is already pressuring margins. Reserved capacity may help unit economics at sufficient utilization, but only if demand and usage justify the lock-in.

### 4. Volatility and lock-in risk
- Demand is explicitly described as **volatile**.
- The contract is a **two-year** commitment.

**Implication:** Volatile demand weakens the case for immediate fixed-capacity lock-in unless there is strong evidence of sustained utilization and commercial necessity.

### 5. Ability to meet enterprise requirements without overcommitting
- Customers want **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.
- The context does not prove that a full two-year reservation is required immediately to satisfy those asks.

**Implication:** The company should test what can be delivered operationally before accepting full financial lock-in.

## Analysis

### Why not commit now

A full commitment now would convert a real but still partly unquantified commercial problem into a certain fixed financial obligation.

That is risky for three reasons:

1. **Runway compression is severe.**  
   Finance’s warning is the clearest hard constraint in the packet: a full commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**. For a mid-market SaaS company with **$31M cash and no debt**, that is a major strategic cost.

2. **Demand volatility undermines confidence in utilization.**  
   Reserved capacity is cheaper than on-demand at expected utilization, but the context also says demand is volatile. Without a utilization threshold or committed customer demand, the company risks paying for capacity it may not fully use.

3. **The commercial upside is directionally clear but not yet quantified enough.**  
   There is **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline**, and enterprise requirements are becoming more stringent. But the context does not establish:
   - how much of that pipeline would be saved by immediate reservation,
   - whether reservation is necessary versus other operational changes,
   - or whether the reliability issue is persistent enough to justify a two-year lock.

### Why not simply defer passively

Preserving optionality should not mean inaction.

There are also meaningful costs to waiting:

1. **Revenue and expansion may be exposed.**  
   If AI feature reliability is undermining expansion, delay could weaken near-term growth.

2. **Enterprise readiness may lag customer expectations.**  
   Data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments are concrete asks. Failure to address them may affect deal progression and expansion credibility.

3. **Gross margin pressure may continue.**  
   If on-demand usage remains high, the company may continue paying a premium while margins remain under pressure.

So the right answer is **active deferral**: preserve optionality while aggressively collecting evidence and improving readiness.

## Staged Options

### Option 1: Commit now to the full two-year reserved contract
**Pros**
- Lower expected infrastructure cost than on-demand at expected utilization.
- May improve reliability and support enterprise commitments.
- Could help defend the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**.

**Cons**
- Creates **$19.2M** total fixed obligation over two years.
- Could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.
- Locks in despite volatile demand and incomplete evidence.

**Board view:** Too much irreversible commitment relative to current uncertainty.

### Option 2: Preserve optionality for six months with explicit triggers
**Pros**
- Protects liquidity and runway while uncertainty is resolved.
- Allows management to test whether reliability improvements materially affect expansion.
- Creates a fact base on utilization, enterprise requirements, and margin impact.

**Cons**
- On-demand remains more expensive at expected utilization.
- Reliability issues may continue to pressure expansion in the interim.
- Requires disciplined execution; delay without milestones would be value-destructive.

**Board view:** Best balance of financial prudence and commercial responsiveness.

### Option 3: Defer without a structured trigger framework
**Pros**
- Maximum short-term cash preservation.

**Cons**
- Fails to address the commercial problem.
- Risks continued margin erosion and pipeline slippage.
- Leaves the board without a decision mechanism.

**Board view:** Not recommended.

## Trigger Thresholds for Reconsidering a Commitment

Because the brief requires trigger thresholds and the context does not provide enough data for numeric utilization or reliability targets, the board should use the following **evidence-based triggers**, limited to known facts:

A return to the board for approval of a reserved commitment should occur if, within the next six months, management can demonstrate all or most of the following:

1. **Commercial trigger:**  
   The **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline** remains contingent on AI reliability and shows credible conversion risk without stronger infrastructure commitments.

2. **Enterprise requirement trigger:**  
   A material share of active enterprise opportunities or expansions explicitly require **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**, and management concludes these cannot be credibly met with the current on-demand approach.

3. **Margin trigger:**  
   Gross margin does not stabilize from the current **66%** level because inference usage continues to outpace pricing changes, and management shows that reserved capacity would likely improve economics at observed usage levels.

4. **Liquidity trigger:**  
   Finance confirms that even with a reserved commitment, the company can absorb the fixed cost without creating unacceptable runway risk under updated planning assumptions.  
   Since the current warning is that runway could fall to **15 months if growth slows**, this trigger is not met today.

5. **Operational trigger:**  
   Management can show that reliability issues are capacity-related rather than primarily product, architecture, or pricing-related. The current context does not isolate the root cause.

These are intentionally conservative. A two-year lock-in should require evidence across commercial, financial, and operational dimensions.
