# Board Strategy Memo: AI Infrastructure Capacity Contract

## Executive Recommendation

**Recommendation: preserve optionality for up to six months rather than commit now to the full two-year reserved AI infrastructure capacity contract.**

Specifically, the board should approve a **conditional deferral**:

1. **Do not sign** the full two-year reserved capacity contract now.
2. **Continue using on-demand capacity** during a bounded review period of up to six months.
3. Require management to return earlier than six months if evidence becomes decisive.
4. Require any future commitment request to meet explicit decision tests on revenue defense, enterprise requirements, gross margin, utilization confidence, and runway impact.

This is not a recommendation to “wait and see.” It is a recommendation to avoid taking a **$19.2M two-year fixed obligation** before management proves that the contract is both **necessary** to protect revenue and **acceptable** under downside liquidity conditions.

The trade-off is real:

- **Commit now:** may improve reliability and support latency commitments while lowering infrastructure cost versus on-demand at expected utilization.
- **Wait up to six months:** preserves cash and flexibility, but risks paying more for capacity and may leave reliability concerns unresolved in the near term.

On current facts, the board has stronger evidence of **lock-in and runway risk** than of the proposition that a full two-year reservation is the required solution to the company’s commercial and operational issues.

---

## Context and Decision Framing

The company has:

- **$31M cash and no debt**
- **$42M ARR**, growing **18% year over year**
- Gross margin decline from **73% to 66%** over three quarters because **inference usage increased faster than pricing changes**
- A reserved capacity option costing **$9.6M per year for two years**
- On-demand capacity costing roughly **1.35x** reserved price at expected utilization
- Enterprise customer asks for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**
- **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** attributed by Sales to AI feature reliability
- A Finance warning that full commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**

The board is not deciding a procurement question in isolation. It is deciding whether to accept a **two-year fixed-cost commitment** in response to a mixed problem involving:

- revenue defense,
- enterprise readiness,
- margin pressure,
- and liquidity risk.

A pure cost framing would be incomplete. Reserved capacity may be cheaper than on-demand at expected utilization, but the company’s margin issue is not only infrastructure price. The stated cause of margin erosion is that **usage increased faster than pricing changes**.

---

## Why the Recommendation Is Deferral, Not Immediate Commitment

### 1. The lock-in is large relative to current liquidity
The reserved contract is **$9.6M per year for two years**, or **$19.2M total**. Finance warns that this could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**. That makes this a balance-sheet decision, not just an operating expense choice.

### 2. Demand volatility raises underutilization risk
The context explicitly states that demand is volatile. That matters because a two-year reservation converts a variable cost into a fixed obligation. Without better utilization confidence, the company risks paying for capacity it does not fully use.

### 3. The commercial case is meaningful but not yet proven
Sales attributes **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** to AI feature reliability. That is important, but it is still pipeline, not closed revenue. The board should not ignore it, but should also not treat it as proof that a two-year reservation is the only or required remedy.

### 4. The enterprise requirement set appears broader than capacity alone
Customers are asking for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**. Reserved capacity may help with **latency** and possibly reliability, but the context does not show that it solves **data residency** or **audit logs**. If those are primary blockers, the company could add fixed cost without unlocking the targeted expansion.

### 5. Margin pressure will not be solved by infrastructure alone
Gross margin fell from **73% to 66%** because inference usage increased faster than pricing changes. Reserved capacity may improve unit cost relative to on-demand, but **without parallel pricing or usage-discipline changes, the company risks converting a variable-cost margin problem into a fixed-cost margin problem**.

## Why Six Months

The six-month period should be treated as a **bounded management review window**, not as a claim that all uncertainty will resolve within six months.

It is justified because it creates time to answer the specific unresolved questions that matter to this decision:

1. Is the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline** truly being blocked by AI reliability?
2. If so, is **reserved capacity** actually required to solve the reliability or latency issue?
3. Are the more important enterprise blockers instead **data residency** and **audit logs**?
4. Can management show a credible path to stabilize gross margin from the current **66%**?
5. Is the runway impact acceptable given the Finance warning of a drop from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**?

If evidence becomes decisive earlier, management should return earlier. If evidence remains inconclusive after six months, that itself is informative and weighs against taking a two-year lock-in on weak proof.

The principal risk in deferring is that the company may spend more on on-demand capacity while still allowing reliability concerns to weaken expansion, making waiting both commercially and economically costly. That is why the review period must be active and tightly governed.

## Explicit Assumptions

Because the context is incomplete, this recommendation depends on the following assumptions:

1. **Reserved capacity would improve reliability and/or latency relative to on-demand.**  
   This is plausible but not directly proven in the context.

2. **On-demand capacity remains available during the review period.**  
   The context provides a price comparison, not a supply constraint.

3. **Demand volatility creates meaningful underutilization risk for a two-year reservation.**  
   Volatility is stated; magnitude is not.

4. **The $6M at-risk expansion pipeline is commercially important but not equivalent to contracted ARR.**  
   It is a sales attribution, not booked revenue.

5. **Reserved capacity may address only part of the enterprise requirement set.**  
   The context does not establish that the contract solves data residency or audit log requirements.

6. **Management can materially improve decision quality within six months if the process is actively managed.**  
   This is an execution assumption, not a fact.

---

## Decision Criteria

Any recommendation to commit should be judged against five criteria:

1. **Runway protection**  
   Can the company absorb the commitment given the warning that runway could fall from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**?

2. **Revenue defense**  
   Does the commitment credibly protect the identified **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**?

3. **Gross margin stabilization**  
   Does management present a credible path to improve or stabilize margin from the current **66%**, recognizing that the stated issue is usage outpacing pricing?

4. **Flexibility under volatility**  
   Is utilization confidence high enough to justify a two-year fixed commitment despite volatile demand?

5. **Enterprise readiness**  
   Does the proposed action address the customer requirements that actually matter, especially distinguishing latency/reliability from data residency and audit logs?

## Strategic Options

### Option A: Commit now to the full two-year reserved contract

**Advantages**
- Lower cost than on-demand at expected utilization
- Potentially stronger reliability and support for latency commitments
- Possible defense of the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**
- Stronger enterprise posture if capacity is the true blocker

**Disadvantages**
- **$19.2M** total lock-in over two years
- Fixed-cost exposure under volatile demand
- Potential runway compression in a slower-growth case
- Does not by itself solve the pricing-versus-usage issue behind margin decline
- May solve only part of the enterprise requirement set

**When this option would be justified**  
Only if management can show that reliability is clearly the binding issue, reserved capacity is required to address it, and the runway impact is acceptable.

### Option B: Preserve optionality for up to six months

**Advantages**
- Avoids immediate two-year lock-in
- Preserves liquidity and downside flexibility
- Allows management to separate capacity needs from broader enterprise-readiness gaps
- Improves decision quality if actively managed

**Disadvantages**
- On-demand costs roughly **1.35x** reserved price at expected utilization
- Reliability concerns may continue to pressure expansion
- Waiting could prove to be the more expensive mistake if reliability is in fact the gating issue

**When this option is justified**  
When the company has not yet proven that a full reservation is necessary, sufficient, and financeable under downside conditions.

## Trigger Thresholds

Because the context does not provide enough data to set new numeric operating targets without inventing facts, the thresholds below are framed as **binary board approval tests** anchored to the known figures: the **$6M at-risk pipeline**, the current **66% gross margin**, and the **24-to-15-month runway warning**.

### Trigger to return to the board early
Management should return before six months if either becomes clear:

1. **Reliability is clearly the binding constraint** on the identified **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**, and reserved capacity is required to support the needed reliability or latency commitments.
2. **Runway risk remains too severe** to support a full commitment under the downside case identified by Finance.

### Trigger to approve a full commitment
Recommend approval only if management can show **all** of the following:

1. The **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline** remains materially at risk specifically because of AI reliability.
2. Reserved capacity is required to support the reliability or latency commitments customers are requesting.
3. Finance concludes the runway impact is acceptable relative to the current **24-to-15-month** warning.
4. Management presents a credible path to gross margin stabilization from the current **66%**, including actions beyond infrastructure procurement.

### Trigger to continue preserving optionality
Continue to defer if **any** of the following are true:

1. The reliability-linked risk to the **$6M pipeline** is not substantiated.
2. Enterprise blockers are primarily **data residency** or **audit logs**, rather than capacity-driven latency or reliability.
3. Margin pressure remains primarily a pricing-versus-usage issue.
4. Runway risk remains close to the downside case flagged by Finance.
5. Utilization confidence remains weak because demand volatility is still high.
