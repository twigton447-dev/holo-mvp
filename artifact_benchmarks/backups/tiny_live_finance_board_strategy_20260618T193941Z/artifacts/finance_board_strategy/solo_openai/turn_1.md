# Board Strategy Memo: AI Infrastructure Capacity Decision

## Recommendation

**Preserve optionality for six months rather than commit immediately to the full two-year reserved AI infrastructure contract.**  
At the same time, use the next six months to run a structured decision process with explicit trigger thresholds tied to revenue protection, gross margin stabilization, and liquidity.

This is the better choice based on the current fact pattern:

- The company has **$31M cash and no debt**, but finance warns that a full commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.
- The reserved contract would create a **fixed $9.6M annual obligation for two years**, which is material relative to current liquidity.
- There is a real commercial risk in waiting: **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** is tied to AI feature reliability, and enterprise buyers are asking for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.
- Gross margin has already declined from **73% to 66% over three quarters** because inference usage rose faster than pricing changes, so locking in capacity without first validating utilization and monetization could harden a margin problem rather than solve it.

The board should therefore favor a **staged approach**: preserve flexibility now, define what must be proven in six months, and commit only if those conditions are met.

---

## Decision Framing

The decision is not simply “cheaper reserved capacity versus more expensive on-demand.” It is a capital allocation choice with three linked questions:

1. **Will reserved capacity materially protect or unlock revenue?**
2. **Will it improve unit economics enough to offset the fixed-cost burden?**
3. **Can the company absorb the two-year lock-in without unacceptable runway compression?**

Because the current context contains both positive and negative signals, an immediate full commitment is not yet justified.

---

## Explicit Assumptions

The following assumptions are used because the context is incomplete:

1. **No partial contract structure is confirmed.** The facts only state a full reserved capacity contract at **$9.6M per year for two years**. Any smaller or phased reservation is uncertain.
2. **On-demand capacity remains available for the next six months.** The context gives a relative cost of **~1.35x reserved price at expected utilization**, but does not state supply constraints.
3. **The $6M expansion pipeline is at risk, not guaranteed.** Reliability issues may affect it, but the exact conversion probability is unknown.
4. **Enterprise requirements matter commercially**, but the memo cannot assume they are impossible to satisfy without a full reservation.
5. **Growth slowdown is a real downside case**, because finance explicitly links the commitment to runway falling from **24 months to 15 months** under slower growth.

These uncertainties support a staged decision rather than an immediate lock-in.

---

## Decision Criteria

The board should approve a full two-year commitment only if most of the following criteria are met within six months:

### 1) Revenue Defense / Expansion
- Evidence that improved AI reliability is necessary to protect or convert the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**.
- Evidence that enterprise asks for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments** require capacity commitments that cannot be met adequately through on-demand usage.

### 2) Gross Margin Stabilization
- Evidence that reserved capacity would help arrest or reverse the decline from **73% to 66% gross margin**.
- Evidence that pricing, packaging, or usage controls are improving the mismatch between inference costs and monetization.

### 3) Liquidity and Runway Protection
- Confidence that the company can absorb the fixed **$9.6M annual** obligation without creating unacceptable downside if growth slows.
- Board comfort that runway compression from **24 months to 15 months** is either unlikely or offset by clear revenue protection.

### 4) Utilization Confidence
- Confidence that expected utilization will be high enough for reserved capacity economics to matter.
- Since demand is described as volatile, the company should not lock in two years of fixed cost without better visibility into actual usage patterns.

## Options Considered

### Option A: Commit now to the full two-year reserved contract
**Pros**
- Lower unit cost than on-demand at expected utilization.
- May improve reliability and support enterprise commitments.
- Could help defend the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**.

**Cons**
- Creates a large fixed obligation: **$19.2M total over two years**.
- Meaningfully increases downside risk to liquidity.
- Could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.
- Locks in before the company has proven demand stability, monetization, or enterprise conversion.

### Option B: Preserve optionality for six months
**Pros**
- Protects cash and strategic flexibility.
- Avoids immediate two-year lock-in while demand remains volatile.
- Allows management to test whether reliability issues truly drive expansion outcomes.
- Gives time to address pricing and margin discipline before adding fixed cost.

**Cons**
- On-demand is **~1.35x** the reserved price at expected utilization.
- Reliability gaps may continue to pressure enterprise expansion.
- Some portion of the **$6M at-risk pipeline** could weaken further.

### Option C: Staged commitment if available
If the vendor offers a smaller or phased reservation, this may be attractive, but **this is not confirmed in the context**. Management should explore it, but the board should not assume it exists.
