# Board Strategy Memo: AI Infrastructure Capacity Commitment

## Recommendation

**Recommend preserving optionality for six months rather than committing immediately to the full two-year reserved AI infrastructure contract.**  
At the same time, management should use the next six months to test whether reliability improvements are sufficiently revenue-protective to justify a fixed two-year cost base, and to define explicit triggers for commitment.

This recommendation is based on three facts in tension:

1. **Reliability matters commercially:** sales attributes **$6M of at-risk expansion pipeline** to AI feature reliability, and enterprise customers are asking for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.
2. **Unit economics are under pressure:** gross margin declined from **73% to 66%** over three quarters because inference usage increased faster than pricing changes.
3. **Balance sheet flexibility matters:** the reserved contract would cost **$9.6M per year for two years**, and finance warns a full commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.

Given those facts, an immediate full commitment appears premature. The company has enough evidence that reliability is strategically important, but not enough evidence in the context provided to conclude that a two-year lock-in is the best risk-adjusted choice today.

## Core Assumptions

These assumptions are explicit because the context is incomplete:

1. **The reserved contract is a fixed commitment** of **$9.6M annually for two years**, regardless of realized demand.
2. **On-demand capacity remains available** over the next six months, albeit at roughly **1.35x reserved price at expected utilization**.
3. **Demand volatility is material**, meaning expected utilization may not match actual utilization.
4. **The $6M expansion pipeline is at risk, not guaranteed lost**, and the exact conversion timing is uncertain.
5. **Enterprise requirements** for data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments may require more than raw capacity alone; the context does not prove that reserved capacity by itself solves all enterprise objections.
6. **No market forecast is available**, so the board should avoid assuming acceleration or deceleration beyond the stated **18% YoY ARR growth**.

## Decision Criteria

The board should evaluate the choice against five criteria:

1. **Runway preservation:** avoid a step-change in fixed cost that materially compresses strategic flexibility.
2. **Revenue defense and expansion support:** improve AI reliability enough to protect the identified **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline**.
3. **Gross margin discipline:** address the recent decline from **73% to 66%**, or at minimum avoid worsening it through poorly utilized committed spend.
4. **Enterprise readiness:** support customer asks for data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments.
5. **Reversibility:** prefer actions that preserve the ability to adapt if demand, pricing, or product adoption changes.

## Strategic Assessment

### Case for committing now

A full reserved commitment offers a lower unit cost than on-demand at expected utilization. Since on-demand is about **1.35x** the reserved price, committing could reduce infrastructure cost per unit if utilization is high enough. It may also improve reliability and support stronger latency commitments, which could help defend the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline** and strengthen enterprise credibility.

This matters because the current pattern is unfavorable: inference usage is rising faster than pricing changes, and gross margin has already fallen by **7 points** over three quarters. If the company expects AI usage to remain structurally high, lower-cost reserved capacity could help stabilize economics.

### Case for waiting six months

The stronger argument from the current evidence is caution. The contract is a **two-year lock-in**, not a short-term optimization. The company has **$31M cash and no debt**, but finance warns the commitment could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**. That is a meaningful loss of flexibility for a mid-market SaaS company whose ARR is **$42M** and growing **18% YoY**, but whose margin trend is deteriorating.

Waiting also avoids a common trap: treating this as a pure cost decision. The real issue is whether the company can translate infrastructure commitment into durable commercial outcomes. The context shows customer demand for reliability-related features, but does not quantify whether reserved capacity alone will secure those outcomes within the next two years.

## Staged Options

### Option 1: Commit now to full two-year reserved capacity
- **Pros:** lower unit cost at expected utilization; stronger basis for reliability and latency commitments.
- **Cons:** fixed **$19.2M total** obligation over two years; runway risk if growth slows; risk of underutilization due to volatile demand.

### Option 2: Preserve optionality for six months and stay on-demand
- **Pros:** protects cash and runway; allows management to validate demand, reliability impact, and enterprise requirements before locking in.
- **Cons:** higher near-term unit cost; possible continued pressure on gross margin; risk to expansion pipeline if reliability remains insufficient.

### Option 3: Six-month decision window with explicit triggers
This is the recommended path. It is effectively Option 2, but with board-approved thresholds that force a decision rather than passive delay.

## Trigger Thresholds for Reassessment in Six Months

Management should return to the board with a commit / do-not-commit recommendation if the following are met or missed:

1. **Pipeline protection trigger:** evidence that the **$6M at-risk expansion pipeline** is converting or being retained specifically due to improved AI reliability.
2. **Margin trigger:** evidence that capacity strategy can stop or improve the decline from **66% gross margin**, rather than merely shifting cost timing.
3. **Enterprise readiness trigger:** demonstrated ability to meet customer asks on **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**.
4. **Runway trigger:** confirmation that a full commitment would not create unacceptable downside relative to the current **24-month runway** warning scenario.

Because the context does not provide numeric thresholds beyond these facts, the board should require management to present measured results against each trigger rather than unsupported forecasts.
