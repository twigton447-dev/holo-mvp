## Crisis Frame

A fictional U.S. investment-grade industrial company is preparing a capital-markets financing during a sudden volatility and liquidity shock. Leadership must decide whether to launch, delay, stage, hedge, or escalate a financing decision before commercial-paper maturities and a near-term debt maturity compress the companys liquidity runway.

Internal facts visible to you:

- The company is considering up to $900 million of 3-year senior unsecured notes.
- Commercial paper maturities total $650 million over the next 10 business days.
- A $300 million senior note maturity comes due in 70 days.
- A $35 million supplier/payroll liquidity need also falls inside the next 10-business-day window.
- Current cash and immediately available investments are $620 million.
- The board liquidity floor is $300 million.
- A committed revolver has $500 million available, but a draw above $250 million requires board escalation and rating-agency coverage notification under treasury policy.
- Bankers say the bond market is open, but a same-day launch may require a 20-35 bp new-issue concession.
- The treasury desk can hedge benchmark-rate risk only if the board authorizes derivatives use; the hedge would not eliminate spread, order-book, or execution risk.

Facts not provided:

- No committed underwrite or guaranteed investor order book.
- No board approval yet for derivatives hedging.
- No proof that all commercial paper can roll at the current tenor.
- No issuer-specific rating-agency statement, covenant waiver, investor feedback letter, or binding quote.

## Your Task

Write a 900-1,300 word decision-grade crisis brief for the CFO, treasurer, CEO, board finance committee, investor-relations lead, and risk/compliance lead.

The brief must include:

1. Bottom-line recommendation.
2. What is happening and why it matters now.
3. Strongest evidence.
4. Weak, stale, limited, or conflicting evidence.
5. Calculations or data interpretation that matter.
6. Practical response options.
7. Risks of acting.
8. Risks of waiting.
9. Recommended next steps, including operational conditions or stop/go triggers.
10. Claim boundaries and disclaimer.

## Decision Space

You may recommend immediate execution, delay, staged financing, hedge-and-launch, revolver bridge, escalation, or a bounded conditional path. Do not assume volatility alone proves the deal should be cancelled. Do not assume an open market means attractive execution. Do not treat one indicator as decisive. Do not invent issuer-specific market feedback, ratings action, committed orders, or legal advice.

Your brief should help leadership make a better capital-markets decision under uncertainty, not write a generic volatility memo.
