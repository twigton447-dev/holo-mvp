# OPTIONS & OPERATIONAL USEFULNESS REVIEW — Turn 4
**Role:** options_operational_usefulness_reviewer
**Objective:** Stress-test the four decision options, risks of acting/waiting, operating gates, and leadership usefulness. *This is not the final brief.*

To the synthesis author: Prior turns established the evidence map and claim boundaries. My job is narrower — to make sure the **options menu is decision-usable**, that each option carries enforceable gates, and that "risks of acting" and "risks of waiting" are asymmetric and source-bounded rather than mirror-image filler.

---

## 1. The four options must be framed as graded, not binary

The task brief mandates adopt / pilot / delay / reject (TASK_BRIEF; S9 frames the decision explicitly as "adopt, pilot, delay, or reject … under uncertainty"). The synthesis author must resist collapsing this into "do it / don't do it." My read of the packet is that only the **middle two options survive** the evidence:

- **Adopt (rapid full expansion):** Not defensible on the packet. No source links remote pulse-ox to reduced admissions, length of stay, or bed-days (S9; TASK_BRIEF). S7 contains a randomized trial finding pulse oximetry "no better than text check-ins" — direct counter-evidence to broad rollout efficacy.
- **Pilot (bounded, instrumented):** Defensible *only* if gated. Urgency is real (S3: ≥1.7M sepsis cases/year, ≥350,000 deaths), and S6 offers a narrow suggestive signal for ambulatory SpO₂ — but neither validates the tool for this population.
- **Delay:** Defensible. The regulatory standard is still moving (S2 draft/non-binding; S4 "proposes"; S5 advisory discussion). Waiting for clearer guidance is rational.
- **Reject:** Over-rotates against S3 urgency. Hard to justify rejecting *all* exploration when deterioration risk is severe.

**Guidance:** Present pilot and delay as the live contenders; show adopt and reject as bracketing positions that the evidence does not support.

---

## 2. Every option needs an operating gate — otherwise the brief is not decision-grade

A leadership brief that lists options without **entry conditions, stop conditions, and equity guards** is not usable. The packet supplies the raw material for concrete gates:

- **Equity gate (hard, source-mandated):** S1, S2, S4, S5, and S8 all corroborate reduced accuracy in darker skin pigmentation — the most consistently sourced thread in the packet. Any pilot must specify how it avoids systematically missing silent deterioration in non-white patients. This is not optional framing; it is the single best-supported risk.
- **"Not standalone" gate:** S1 explicitly says patients should not rely only on a pulse oximeter; symptoms and worsening status matter. Any option must pair the device with symptom escalation pathways, not replace clinical contact. Note S7's RCT signal: text check-ins matched device performance — so the human-contact arm is not a nicety, it may be the active ingredient.
- **No-invasive-fallback constraint:** S8 notes the reliable alternative (arterial blood gas) is invasive and cannot be done remotely. This means a remote pulse-ox program has **no remote confirmatory test** when readings are doubtful — a structural limit the gates must acknowledge.

**Guidance:** Each option should carry explicit stop/escalation criteria. A pilot without a pre-declared failure threshold and equity-stratified monitoring is indistinguishable from uncontrolled adoption.

---

## 3. Risks of acting vs. risks of waiting — keep them asymmetric and sourced

The prior-turn audit failures came from overclaiming ("fda approved," "solves hospital capacity"). The options section is where that risk re-enters, because "risk of waiting" tempts the author to imply the tool *would* have helped. Bound both sides:

**Risks of acting (well-sourced):**
- Missed deterioration in darker-skinned patients due to device inaccuracy (S1, S2, S4, S5, S8).
- False reassurance from a tool FDA says should not be relied on alone (S1).
- Resource diversion to an intervention with contradictory benefit evidence (S7).
- Building program on an evolving, non-final regulatory standard (S2, S4, S5) — possible rework when guidance finalizes.

**Risks of waiting (must NOT overclaim):**
- The clinical urgency is genuine and severe (S3). Delay leaves the underlying deterioration-detection gap unaddressed.
- **Boundary:** The author may say delay leaves a *need* unmet (S3); the author may **not** say delay forgoes proven capacity relief or proven early detection, because no packet source establishes either (S9; S7 is contradictory; S6 is a stale preprint).

**Guidance:** The asymmetry is the point — risk of acting is concretely sourced; risk of waiting is real only as unmet need (S3), not as foregone benefit. Do not let the two sides appear symmetric.

---

## 4. The "calculations / data interpretation" requirement is a trap

The task brief requires a calculations section, but the packet has only one quantitative anchor: S3's sepsis figures (≥1.7M/year; ≥350,000 deaths). The synthesis author should:

- Use S3 to size the *problem* (magnitude of deterioration risk), explicitly labeling it as denominator/context, not as a pulse-ox efficacy estimate.
- **Avoid fabricating** admission-reduction, bed-day, or sensitivity/specificity numbers — none exist in the packet. Any invented figure is an overclaim.
- Interpret S6's "ear-canal faster than finger in breath-hold testing" as a directional, non-clinical signal only — not a performance metric for program design (S6).

**Guidance:** Frame the data interpretation as "what we can and cannot quantify." The honest calculation is that the packet sizes the need but not the solution.

## 5. Practical next steps must be executable, not aspirational

For leadership usefulness, next steps should be things this audience (CMO, regulatory lead, clinical ops, quality, implementation) can actually authorize:

- Regulatory lead: track the draft guidance toward finalization before scaling (S2, S4, S5) — explicitly *not* treat current draft as a green light.
- Quality/clinical ops: if piloting, pre-register equity-stratified outcome monitoring given the pigmentation accuracy concern (S1, S2, S4, S5, S8).
- Clinical ops: pair any device arm with a symptom-based escalation pathway and consider a check-in comparator given S7's RCT signal.
- CMO: hold the line on claim boundaries internally — the program is exploratory triage support, not a capacity solution (S9).

## 6. Usefulness checklist for the synthesis author

The final brief is decision-grade only if a reader can answer: *Which option, under what gate, with what stop condition?* Confirm the brief:
1. Names pilot and delay as the live options, adopt/reject as brackets (S9).
2. Attaches an equity gate to every active option (S1, S2, S4, S5, S8).
3. Keeps risk-of-waiting as unmet need (S3), never foregone proven benefit (S9, S7).
4. Sizes the problem with S3 without inventing efficacy numbers.
5. Repeats the no-overclaim boundaries (no "FDA approved," no "solves capacity") and includes the required disclaimer.

**Net:** The options menu is the highest-risk section for boundary violations and the highest-value section for leadership. Graded options + enforceable gates + asymmetric risk framing are what convert this from a literature summary into a decision aid.
