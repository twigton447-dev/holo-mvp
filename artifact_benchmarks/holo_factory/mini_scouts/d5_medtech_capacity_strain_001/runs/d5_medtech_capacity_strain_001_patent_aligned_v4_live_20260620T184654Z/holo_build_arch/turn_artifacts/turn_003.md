# Contradiction / Uncertainty / Source-Fidelity Review Findings

## 1. Core decision claim is under-supported
- The packet supports that leadership is considering remote pulse-ox expansion during respiratory and sepsis-related capacity strain, but it does **not** show that remote pulse-ox monitoring reduces admissions, preserves beds, or safely substitutes for inpatient monitoring (TASK_BRIEF; S9_DERIVED_EVIDENCE_PRESSURE_TABLE).
- Any final brief should avoid “capacity relief” language unless framed as a hypothesis to test in a controlled pilot, not an established effect.

## 2. FDA material must be bounded tightly
- FDA sources support that pulse oximeters estimate oxygen saturation and pulse rate, but FDA cautions that patients should not rely only on a pulse oximeter; symptoms and worsening status remain important (S1_FDA_PULSE_OX_PAGE_2025).
- The 2025 FDA guidance source is explicitly **draft, not for implementation, and non-binding**; it cannot be described as approval, clearance, a mandate, or a finalized standard (S2_FDA_DRAFT_GUIDANCE_2025).
- The FDA press release says current scientific evidence suggests accuracy differences between lighter and darker skin pigmentation and that proposed recommendations aim to generate more representative studies; this is not proof that the issue is fixed (S4_FDA_PRESS_RELEASE_2025).
- The 2024 advisory meeting discussed study methods, data needs, labeling, and concerns about reduced accuracy in darker skin pigmentation; it is discussion context, not a binding regulatory decision (S5_FDA_ADVISORY_MEETING_2024).

## 3. Accuracy/equity risk is strong, but wording must not overreach
- Strongest cross-source signal: pulse-ox accuracy can be affected by skin pigmentation, and FDA is actively addressing performance across skin tones (S1; S2; S4; S5).
- Avoid categorical claims such as “all devices are racially biased” or “the technology is unusable.” Better: “FDA and other packet sources identify ongoing concerns that pulse oximeters may be less accurate or less reliable for people with darker skin pigmentation” (S1; S4; S5; S8_VERYWELL_LIMITED_EXPLAINER_2024).
- Also avoid implying that ABG testing is a practical mass substitute; S8 only says arterial blood gas testing is invasive, not that it is operationally feasible for remote triage.

## 4. Sepsis evidence supports urgency, not pulse-ox sufficiency
- CDC supports the seriousness of sepsis: at least 1.7 million U.S. adults develop sepsis annually, and at least 350,000 die during hospitalization or are discharged to hospice (S3_CDC_SEPSIS_CONTEXT_2026).
- But the packet does not establish that SpO2 monitoring detects sepsis deterioration reliably or early. Final wording should say sepsis burden raises the stakes of missed deterioration, not that pulse-ox is a sepsis detection tool (S3; S1).

## 5. COVID-era and consumer sources are weak/contradictory
- S6 is an older COVID-era arXiv preprint; it argues ambulatory SpO2 monitoring was needed for delayed COVID hypoxemia and reports faster ear-canal response than finger response in breath-hold testing. This is suggestive technical evidence only, not current clinical adoption proof or hospital-capacity evidence (S6_DAVIES_ARXIV_EAR_OX_2020).
- S7 is secondary health journalism and explicitly reports conflicting conclusions: one randomized trial found pulse oximetry no better than text check-ins, while another viewpoint argued pulse oximeters could help detect silent hypoxia (S7_HEALTH_HOME_PULSE_OX_CONFLICT_2022).
- S8 is a consumer-health explainer useful for communication framing but limited as clinical evidence (S8).

## 6. Data interpretation boundaries
- A rough burden calculation from CDC figures is 350,000 / 1.7 million ≈ 20.6%, but the numerator combines deaths during hospitalization and discharge to hospice, so it should be presented only as a crude severity indicator, not a case-fatality rate (S3).
- Missing from the packet: local bed occupancy, admission-avoidance rates, false-negative/false-positive rates, alert burden, staffing model, escalation compliance, subgroup accuracy statistics, and outcome data for sepsis-specific remote monitoring. These absences should drive a pilot-or-delay framing, not full rapid adoption.

## 7. Suggested decision boundary for synthesis
- Most source-faithful recommendation: **pilot with safeguards** or **delay full-scale expansion pending protocol, equity, and escalation controls**.
- Avoid “reject” unless framed as rejecting **uncontrolled rapid expansion**, because FDA does not say pulse oximeters should not be used; FDA says they should not be relied on alone (S1).
- Avoid “adopt rapidly” unless limited to adjunctive use with symptom checks, clinician escalation, device labeling review, equity monitoring, and explicit non-reliance on SpO2 alone (S1; S2; S4; S5).
