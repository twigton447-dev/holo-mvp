## highest_value_flaw

The artifact's most exploitable remaining weakness is the **six-month timeline itself — it is asserted but not justified**. The memo recommends a six-month decision window without explaining why six months is the right interval rather than three months or twelve months. A hostile critic can attack this as an arbitrary anchor that may be too long (pipeline erodes, margin worsens) or too short (insufficient time to validate root cause and deal-level pipeline). The memo also does not address what happens at the end of six months if the prerequisite gates are not met — it implies the board reconvenes, but it does not state what the default action is if the gates remain unresolved. This creates a logical gap: the trigger framework tells the board when to commit, but not what to do if the evidence never arrives.

## next_role_objective

**Role: Hostile Critic — skeptical executive or domain expert**

The Hostile Critic's objective is to pressure-test the repaired artifact by identifying arguments that a skeptical CFO, board member, or infrastructure expert would raise in a real decision meeting. The critique should not be a general review. It should identify the specific claims, numbers, or logical structures in the artifact that are most vulnerable to challenge and construct the strongest possible objection to each.

The Hostile Critic should focus on:

1. **Attacking the six-month timeline** — why is six months the right window? What is the cost of waiting in concrete terms using only the given numbers? What is the default if gates are not met by month six?
2. **Challenging the prerequisite gate structure** — is it realistic to confirm operational root cause within six months? What does "confirmed" mean in practice? Who confirms it and by what standard?
3. **Pressing on the on-demand cost exposure during the wait** — the memo acknowledges on-demand costs 1.35x reserved at expected utilization. Over six months, what is the incremental cost of waiting? The artifact does not calculate this. A hostile critic should demand the number.
4. **Questioning whether optionality is actually preserved** — if the company waits six months and then decides to commit, is the reserved contract still available at the same price? The memo assumes the option to commit later exists, but the source context does not confirm this.
5. **Probing the enterprise requirement gap** — the memo says enterprise customers are asking for data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments. It does not say these can be delivered without reservation. A hostile critic should ask: what is the company telling enterprise prospects right now, and what happens to those deals during the six-month window?
