# Board Strategy Memo: AI Infrastructure Capacity Commitment

## Executive recommendation

**Recommendation: preserve optionality for the next six months, while preparing a trigger-based path to commit earlier if specific commercial and operating thresholds are met.**

This is not a recommendation to defer the issue passively. It is a recommendation to **run a staged decision process** because the company faces two real and opposing risks:

- **Committing now** improves reliability and enterprise credibility, and may help defend a **$6 million at-risk expansion pipeline** tied to AI feature reliability.
- **Committing now** also adds a large fixed obligation: **$9.6 million per year for two years**, which Finance warns could reduce runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.

Given the company’s **$31 million cash**, **no debt**, **$42 million ARR**, and recent **gross margin decline from 73% to 66%**, the board should avoid treating this as a simple unit-cost optimization. It is a **revenue defense and strategic flexibility decision**. On the current facts, the balance favors preserving flexibility for six months, but only with a disciplined near-term plan to test whether reserved capacity is necessary to protect enterprise expansion and product commitments.

## Decision framing

The core question is not “reserved versus on-demand cost.” The core question is:

**Does locking in two years of reserved accelerator capacity now create enough revenue protection, reliability improvement, and enterprise credibility to justify the cash and lock-in risk?**

The answer is uncertain because the context shows both:

1. **Commercial pressure to improve AI reliability**
   - Enterprise customers are asking for **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments** before expanding contracts.
   - Sales attributes **$6 million of at-risk expansion pipeline** to AI feature reliability.

2. **Financial pressure to avoid fixed-cost lock-in**
   - Reserved capacity would cost **$9.6 million annually for two years**.
   - Finance warns a full commitment could cut runway from **24 months to 15 months if growth slows**.
   - Demand is volatile, even though on-demand currently costs about **1.35x** the reserved price at expected utilization.

## Explicit assumptions

The recommendation depends on the following assumptions, all grounded in the context and labeled where incomplete:

1. **The reserved contract is a full two-year commitment** at **$9.6 million per year**.
2. **On-demand capacity remains available** during the next six months. The context does not state supply risk, so this remains uncertain.
3. **Reliability issues are materially linked to infrastructure capacity**, because sales ties at-risk expansion to AI feature reliability. The exact causal share is uncertain.
4. **Enterprise requirements extend beyond raw capacity**, since customers also request **data residency, audit logs, and latency commitments**. Reserved capacity alone may not satisfy all expansion blockers.
5. **Expected utilization favors reserved pricing**, since on-demand is about **1.35x** reserved at expected utilization, but volatility means realized savings are uncertain.
6. **Runway preservation matters strategically**, because the company has **no debt** and would absorb the commitment from cash.

## Decision criteria

The board should evaluate the choice against five criteria:

1. **Revenue defense**
   - Does the decision materially improve the probability of protecting the **$6 million at-risk expansion pipeline**?

2. **Reliability and enterprise readiness**
   - Does it enable credible commitments on **latency**, and support broader enterprise requirements including **data residency** and **audit logs**?

3. **Gross margin trajectory**
   - Can the decision help arrest the decline from **73% to 66% gross margin**, either through lower unit cost or better pricing support?

4. **Runway and balance-sheet resilience**
   - Is the fixed commitment acceptable given **$31 million cash**, **no debt**, and Finance’s warning about runway falling from **24 to 15 months if growth slows**?

5. **Strategic flexibility**
   - Does the company preserve room to adapt if demand, product architecture, or customer requirements change over the next six months?

## Recommendation rationale

### Why not commit immediately

A full commitment now appears premature for three reasons:

- **Cash concentration risk is high.** A **$9.6 million annual obligation** is large relative to **$31 million cash**.
- **The contract creates two-year lock-in.** The context explicitly warns against ignoring strategic lock-in.
- **Capacity may not solve the full enterprise expansion problem.** Customers also require **data residency** and **audit logs**, so infrastructure reservation alone may not unlock expansions.

### Why not simply wait without action

Doing nothing for six months would also be a mistake:

- The company already has **gross margin pressure** from inference usage outpacing pricing changes.
- Sales sees **$6 million** of expansion at risk due to reliability.
- Waiting may weaken product commitments and enterprise credibility.

Therefore, the right answer is **structured optionality**, not passive delay.

## Staged decision framework

### Stage 1: Next six months
Preserve optionality while gathering evidence on whether reserved capacity is necessary and sufficient to defend revenue and improve margins.

### Stage 2: Trigger-based board decision
Return to the board before six months, or earlier if triggers are met, to decide whether to sign the two-year contract.

## Trigger thresholds for commitment

The board should authorize management to commit earlier only if most of the following are demonstrated:

- Clear evidence that AI reliability is the primary blocker to the **$6 million at-risk expansion pipeline**.
- Evidence that reserved capacity is required to support credible **latency commitments** for enterprise customers.
- A plan showing how **data residency** and **audit logs** will also be addressed, since capacity alone is insufficient.
- Confidence that the runway impact remains acceptable relative to current cash and growth conditions.
- Evidence that expected utilization is likely to be high enough that the **1.35x on-demand premium** is materially costly if the company waits.

Because the context does not provide operating metrics, exact numeric trigger thresholds beyond the provided figures cannot be responsibly invented.
