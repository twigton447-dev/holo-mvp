The $184,750 Missing PO Window

The packet looked completely safe on the surface. The vendor was real. The banking change had been fully verified via strong internal controls, including a portal submission with multi-factor authentication and a successful voice callback to the phone number on file since 2021. Two corporate approvers had signed off, and the vendor master file was updated before the invoice arrived.

So 10 out of 11 AI systems approved the payment.

But the invoice referenced a specific purchase order: PO FW-HFS-2026-04. That purchase order artifact was entirely missing from the packet. Without it, there was no documentary proof of approved scope, amount ceiling, or formal procurement authority.

The payment route was verified. The payment itself was completely unauthorized. Standard AI cleared the loud risk. Holo found the quiet one.

The Bug: Gate Collapse

Most AI systems do not fail because they are dumb. They fail because they answer the nearest question.

In this test, the nearest question was: Is this bank account change legitimate?
The harder, ultimate question was: Is this payment authorized?

Ten out of eleven architectures answered the first question and stopped. They treated a cleared fraud check as if it cleared the entire action. But that is not how enterprise business works. A payment can pass vendor verification and still fail authorization.

Why We Benchmark Differently

Most benchmarks are flawed because humans pre-label the answers. A human engineer reads a packet, creates a static answer key, and measures how often an AI agrees with them.

The problem is that humans have the exact same blind spots as AI.

In fact, we originally labeled our own missing PO packet as ALLOW. We missed the missing paperwork on our own first manual pass. It was only after running the tests blind and reading the adversarial model traces that the system revealed our mistake to us.

That is why our benchmark correct answers do not exist before the runtime. Ground truth is derived by an independent 5-turn adversarial Judge panel that reviews the packet alongside all 11 cross-vendor reasoning traces after the execution. You cannot game an answer key that has not been written yet.
