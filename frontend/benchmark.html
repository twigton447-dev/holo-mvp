Action Boundary Benchmark  ·  June 2026  ·  Version 1.0

10 of 11 architectures
approved the fraud.

In a controlled, hash-locked benchmark, 10 out of 11 standard enterprise AI architectures approved a simulated $184,750 unauthorized payment. Only one caught the critical gap: the missing purchase order that proved the payment was never authorized.

The models were identical. The architecture was not.

The Precision Paradox: Why Standard AI Fails at the Action Boundary
  1. ↓Semantic Surrender (False Negative): The model accepts surface authority — an executive signature, a clean compliance log — and waves through an unauthorized action.
  2. ↑Rigid Auditor (False Positive): The model becomes hyper-literal, escalates over minor or explainable issues, and freezes legitimate operations.
Context

Why this matters.

Enterprise teams are rapidly moving from AI that assists to AI that acts. When an AI agent wants to release a payment, change access rights, execute a contract, or trigger a workflow, the final decision point — the action boundary — becomes the highest-risk moment in the system.

Most current approaches rely on Layer 1 controls: identity registration, access policies, basic guardrails, and surface-level checks. These are necessary. But they are not sufficient.

This benchmark demonstrates a structural gap that Layer 1 controls consistently miss. Most AI systems do not fail because they are unintelligent. They fail because they answer the nearest question instead of the right question.

In the $184,750 test, the nearest question was: "Is this bank account change legitimate?" The harder, ultimate question was: "Is this payment actually authorized?" Standard AI setups treat a cleared fraud check as if it clears the entire action. They check the passport at the gate, but they do not interrogate whether the person — or agent — should actually be allowed through with what they are trying to do.

The Structural Gap

Layer 1 is not enough.

Current enterprise AI deployments rely heavily on Layer 1 controls: identity and access management, policy enforcement, and basic guardrails and compliance checks. These are essential. However, they operate at the level of registration and surface validation. They do not perform deep, evidence-based interrogation of the full context right before an irreversible action executes.

Holo Engine provides the missing Layer 2 — a runtime adversarial judgment layer that forces structured cross-examination and returns a binding ALLOW or ESCALATE verdict based on explicit documentary evidence.

Holo does not replace Layer 1. It makes the final commitment point trustworthy.

Failure Mode Analysis

Why solo models fail.

Three structurally distinct failure modes. A single model cannot cover all three because the same reasoning loop that generates a concern is the loop that resolves it.

GPT-5.4
Detection Failure
Accepts a plausible narrative and does not cross-reference historical data that contradicts it. Mistakes a smooth explanation for factual truth.
Claude Sonnet 4.6
Persuasion Failure
Correctly identifies the risk signal in an early turn, then talks itself out of escalating because the surrounding packet looks professional and clean.
All Solo Models
Contextual Brittleness
When presented with resolving evidence buried in legal documentation, solo models misread standard institutional mechanics and block valid transactions. The same model that misses a hidden gap will panic at a properly documented exception.

Holo's adversarial council separates initial assessment from pressure testing. The Governor operates on verified evidence, not rhetorical persuasion.

Methodology

How we test.

Model Roster Parity

Solo baselines use the exact same model versions inside the Holo run. The variable being tested is adjudication architecture — isolated single-model judgment versus shared adversarial review.

Generous Solo Baselines

Solo models received the full evidence packet, policy framing, and decision context. They were not deprived of data. They failed because they lacked adversarial evidence collision.

Pre-Registration & Hash-Locking

Every benchmark run is pre-registered before execution. Payloads, harness configuration, and model rosters are SHA-256 fingerprinted before verdicts are generated. Verdict generation cannot alter the registered payload.

A result is published here only after passing ten integrity gates:

1
Verdict Stability
Holds across randomized model and role assignment seeds. One run is noise.
2
Correct Catch Reason
The model cites the intended structural signal, not a coincidental fluke.
3
No Answer Key in Context
No labeled field directly identifies the disqualifying condition.
4
Clean Trace
The turn-by-turn audit is readable by a technical outsider without explanation.
5
One-Sentence Takeaway
The proof point is expressible in plain operational language.
6
No Infrastructure Contamination
No API timeouts or adapter failures affected the run.
7
Schema Validity
Packet schema is complete and machine-validated before execution begins.
8
Hash-Locked Payload
SHA-256 fingerprint of the evidence packet, harness config, and model roster generated before verdict.
9
Independent Judge Adjudication
Judge panel reviews verdict and cited evidence behind an architectural firewall with full trace anonymization.
10
Ledger Registration
Final packet hash, verdict, and trace archive registered to the audit ledger. Post-registration edits are detectable.
