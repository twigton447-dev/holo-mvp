The Action Boundary Benchmark

Hardening AI for Autonomous Execution

When people talk about AI safety, they usually mean stopping models from saying bad things.

In an enterprise, that is not the main risk.

The real risk is what the system does.

Every automated workflow has a last checkpoint before money moves, access is granted, a purchase is made, or some other irreversible action happens. We call that moment the action boundary.

If an AI hallucinates in a chat window, it is annoying. If it hallucinates at the action boundary, you lose money, create liability, or trigger the wrong real-world outcome.

That is what this benchmark tests.

There is now a second version of the same problem earlier in the process. AI is not only being trusted to act. It is also being trusted to produce the work people rely on before anyone acts.

That work can fail too.

A system can produce something that looks careful and complete, but still fail the simple test: did it actually prove the required sources, approvals, and limits?

That is why this benchmark has two lanes.

HoloVerify

Runtime decisions

Should the action execute or not?

HoloBuild

High-stakes work product

Can the system produce something complete enough and well-supported enough to trust?

The two lanes are related, but they do not make the same claim.

Key Takeaway

Kit A showed that solo frontier models will approve a payment even when the purchase order that proves authority is missing.

Kit B showed that solo frontier models will treat vague or stale policy language as if it were current approval.

The newer HoloBuild lane showed something else: even a very strong solo frontier model can understand the task and still fail to finish high-stakes work cleanly under real production limits.

Holo's job is simple: before action or reliance, prove the required checks are complete.

The First Proof Case

Solo Frontier Models Approved the Payment. Holo Stopped It.

We ran the same invoice payment request through all five architecture families.

The numbers, bank routing profiles, and communication logs stayed exactly the same.

A $184,750 payment looked ready to send. The vendor was real. The bank details matched. Multi-factor authentication passed. Two managers signed off.

It looked normal.

But the invoice referenced a purchase order that did not exist. Without it, there was no proof of approved spending authority.

Solo frontier models saw the visible banking checks and treated the workflow as complete.

Holo asked the harder question:

Does this payment actually have authority to execute?

The Two Benchmark Lanes

Two Kinds of Trust, Two Kinds of Failure

The same trust problem appears twice.

First, it appears at the moment of execution. A system is about to send money, grant access, place an order, or approve something irreversible.

That is the HoloVerify lane.

Second, it appears earlier, when someone is about to rely on work AI produced. A document may look polished and complete, but still fail to prove what it claims.

That is the HoloBuild lane.

Opus 4.8-Facing HoloBuild Findings

We Raise the Bar Until They Break

The Opus 4.8-facing lane is separate from Kit A and Kit B.

It does not test runtime ALLOW / ESCALATE verdicts. It tests whether a high-stakes decision document can survive source review, fixed validation rules, claim limits, and blind comparison.

Early in this benchmark, solo frontier models were failing by large margins. That was useful, but it was not the final test.

When stronger models started closing the gap, we did two things:

  • Harder tests
    We made the tests harder and more complex.
  • Better gates
    We taught the Governor what the new domain actually required.

Our goal is not to find models that already pass. Our goal is to break them, including Holo itself.

When a model narrows the gap, we widen it again. We keep raising the difficulty until we find the breaking point. That is how the benchmark improves.

Architecture / Model Economics

Turning Intelligence Into Boundary Discipline

The newer Opus 4.8-facing HoloBuild lane showed a harder truth: stronger models narrow the gap, but they do not remove the boundary.

When that happens, we respond by making the tests harder, tightening the rules, and teaching the Governor what the new domain requires. That is how we keep finding the real breaking point.

Holo's value is not just that one model beats another.

Holo's value is the governed process itself: adversarial roles, source review, fixed validation rules, repair checks, and a Governor that makes the final call.

In D11, Holo gained +18.4 strict-score points and +24 action-boundary points over fresh solo Opus 4.8 while using about 1.9x total tokens. That is not a universal cost claim. It does suggest that better reliability at the action boundary may not require ten or fifty times more budget.

At the action boundary, a model can sound right and still fail the process.

Holo turns model intelligence into disciplined, audited decision work.

The Core Insight

Proof vs. Plausibility

The models did not fail because they lacked facts.

They failed because they accepted the wrong thing as proof.

  • Reference
    A reference is not an approval.
  • Checklist
    A checklist is not an approval.
  • Operations
    Operational completeness is not authority.
  • Document polish
    A polished document is not the same thing as a trustworthy one.

When AI sees a workflow or document that looks mostly complete, it tends to keep it moving.

Holo does something else.

It checks whether the required proof is actually there.

If the proof is missing, Holo escalates.

If the proof is complete, Holo allows.

That is why this benchmark is not really about whether a model can sound smart.

It is about whether a system can tell the difference between something that looks plausible and something that is actually safe to trust.

The Standard

Ten-Gate Publication

To prevent cherry-picking, every public benchmark asset must clear ten distinct gates before it is counted in the registry. This ensures all results can be verified and reproduced from disk.

  1. Schema Validation
    Verified against native enterprise protocol definitions.
  2. Payload Scrubbing
    All tracking metadata and timestamps are completely removed.
  3. Packet Freezing
    The immutable data packet is saved to disk and locked.
  4. Prompt Hash Generation
    Exact system instructions are hashed to keep context static.
  5. Blinded Trace Capture
    Executed at a locked, low temperature of 0.1.
  6. Combined Hash Integration
    Packet and prompt hashes merge into a single root signature.
  7. Independent Adjudication
    An isolated Judge grades final trace outputs.
  8. Behavior Labeling
    Models are tagged based on core error comprehension.
  9. Runtime Hash Verification
    Active files check themselves against the locked roots.
  10. Ledger Publication Lock
    Marked as permanent benchmark_locked assets.

Engineering Integrity

The Tuning Loop

We do not design our safety filters in a vacuum. To build absolute credibility, we explicitly track and test against our own engineering setbacks.

During early discovery in our Financial Reporting domain (Domain 8), the Holo Governor encountered complex private equity corporate sheets (PE-CONSOLIDATION-001). The system initially tried to force these complex financial close documents through our standard Accounts Payable filter, resulting in severe false positive instability — alternating between over-blocking valid entries and throwing system routing errors.

1. Isolate & Freeze

We immediately isolated and froze the failing packets to prevent post-hoc bias.

2. Deploy the Patch

We deployed a patch (Commit b8f1ded) to introduce specialized context routing.

3. Rerun Regression Tests

We re-ran regression tests, achieving a stable 5/5 pass track without degrading core AP fraud boundaries. Note: Regression runs validate patches; they do not carry new benchmark credit unless separately frozen and adjudicated.

Integrity Standard

Losses, Repairs, and Reruns

The industry standard for AI benchmarks is dilution. If a system fails a test, the vendor runs 90 more easy tests to make the failure rate look like a rounding error.

Holo does not dilute losses. We freeze them.

When our v2 patch failed to clear our two false positives, we didn’t quietly overwrite the run. We locked the v2 artifacts in our matrix, marked the precision fix as a failure, and recorded the status as superseded. We are running our v3 patch regression exclusively on that exact same frozen 20-packet set.

We don’t improve by burying losses. We improve by turning exact failures into structural Governor infrastructure, rerunning the frozen failure, and testing siblings to prove the judgment generalized.
